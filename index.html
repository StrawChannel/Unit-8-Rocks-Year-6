The Rock Cycle: Earth's Geological Engine

The rock cycle is a dynamic planetary recycling system operating across temporal scales from milliseconds (volcanic eruptions) to billions of years (continental formation). This ceaseless transformation network connects Earth's deep mantle processes with surface weathering, driven by both endogenic (internal) and exogenic (external) energy systems.

Lithospheric Dynamics

Plate tectonics drives crustal recycling through subduction (0.5-8 cm/yr) and seafloor spreading (2-16 cm/yr)

Energy Transfer

Geothermal gradient (25-30°C/km) enables metamorphism, while solar radiation powers surface weathering at 164 W/m²

Material Flux

Global sediment yield: 20 Gt/yr, with 0.3 Gt/yr subducted into mantle

Paleogeological Evidence

The Isua Greenstone Belt (Greenland) contains 3.8 Ga metasediments demonstrating early cycle operation. Jack Hills zircons (4.4 Ga) show granitic magmatism within 160 Myr of Earth's formation.

Key Parameters

• Mantle convection rate: 1-20 cm/yr

• Continental crust density: 2.7-2.8 g/cm³

• Oceanic crust production: 3.3 km²/yr

Igneous Systems: Magmatic Differentiation

Igneous rocks form through partial melting of mantle peridotite (0.1-20% melt fractions) and subsequent magma evolution via Bowen's Reaction Series. Texture depends on cooling rate (10⁻¹°C/yr for plutons vs 10³°C/min for pyroclastics).

Crystallization Dynamics

Mafic magmas (45-55% SiO₂) follow discontinuous series: olivine → pyroxene → amphibole. Felsic magmas (>65% SiO₂) exhibit ternary minima in Qz-Ab-Or system. Pegmatites form from H₂O-rich residual melts ( <1% of magma volume).

Large Igneous Provinces

The Columbia River Basalts (16.7 Ma) cover 163,700 km² with >174,300 km³ lava. Deccan Traps (66 Ma) erupted 1.3 million km³ basalt over 30,000 years, potentially influencing K-Pg extinction.

Magma Properties

• Basaltic viscosity: 10²-10³ Pa·s

• Rhyolitic SiO₂: 72-76 wt%

• Mantle potential T: 1280-1400°C

Sedimentary Systems: Stratigraphic Archives

Sedimentary rocks preserve Earth's surface history through bedform architecture and chemostratigraphic signals. Clastic sediments follow Udden-Wentworth grading:

  • Rudites (>2 mm conglomerates)
  • Arenites (62.5 μm-2 mm sandstones)
  • Lutites (<62.5 μm mudrocks)

Diagenesis

Mechanical compaction reduces porosity from 40% → 10% at 2 km depth. Quartz cementation initiates at 70-80°C (2-3 km burial)

Biogenic Deposition

Global CaCO₃ production: 5 Gt/yr. Cretaceous chalk contains >90% coccolithophores (Emiliania huxleyi)

Chemogenic Processes

Evaporite sequences follow Usiglio's sequence: calcite → gypsum → halite. Banded iron formations (3.8-1.8 Ga) record atmospheric oxygenation events through hematite-magnetite alternations.

Deposition Rates

• Fluvial: 0.1-10 mm/yr

• Pelagic clay: 0.1-1 cm/kyr

• Turbidites: 10-100 cm/event

Metamorphic Systems: Mineral Reequilibration

Metamorphism occurs along geotherms (15-30°C/km) through solid-state reactions. Phase changes follow Gibbs' Phase Rule: F = C - P + 2. Barrovian zones progress from chlorite → garnet → kyanite → sillimanite.

Metamorphic Facies

• Zeolite (T < 200°C): laumontite+prehnite
• Blueschist (P > 0.6 GPa): glaucophane+lawsonite
• Eclogite (>1.4 GPa): omphacite+garnet

Orogenic Metamorphism

The Himalayas exhibit inverted metamorphic gradients: kyanite-grade gneiss (15-20 km depth) overthrusts biotite schists, preserving peak P-T conditions of 10-12 kbar/650-750°C.

Reaction Kinetics

• Nucleation rate: 10³-10⁶/cm³s

• Activation energy: 200-500 kJ/mol

• Dry diffusion: 10⁻¹⁹ m²/s
