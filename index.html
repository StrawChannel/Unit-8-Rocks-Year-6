The Rock Cycle: Earth's Eternal Recycler

The rock cycle is the continuous process of rock transformation that shapes our planet's surface and interior. Over geological timescales, rocks transition between three main types through various physical and chemical processes.

Historical Significance

The rock cycle has shaped human civilization through:

  • Flint tools in prehistoric times
  • Marble sculptures of antiquity
  • Modern concrete infrastructure

Key Concepts

• Timescale: Millions of years

• Energy Sources: Earth's internal heat & solar energy

• Plate Tectonics: Primary driver

Igneous Rocks: Born of Fire

Igneous rocks form through the cooling and crystallization of molten rock material, either below the surface as intrusive rocks or on the surface as extrusive volcanic rocks.

Formation Process

When magma cools slowly beneath Earth's crust, it forms coarse-grained intrusive rocks like granite. Rapid cooling of lava at the surface creates fine-grained extrusive rocks like basalt.

Economic Importance

Igneous rocks are essential in:

  • Construction (granite countertops)
  • Abrasives (pumice stones)
  • Technology (quartz in electronics)

Fast Facts

• Ocean crust is 99% basalt

• Granite forms continental cores

• Obsidian fractures atomically

Metamorphic Rocks: Transformed by Pressure

Existing rocks transformed by heat and pressure without melting, creating new structures and mineral compositions.

Metamorphic Textures

Foliated: Layered minerals (schist)
Non-foliated: Uniform texture (marble)

Human Utilization

  • Slate roofing tiles
  • Marble architecture
  • Talcum powder production

Metamorphic Grades

Low: 200-320°C (slate)
Medium: 320-450°C (schist)
High: 450-900°C (gneiss)
